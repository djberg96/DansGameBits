Although saddened by the loss of his cavalry commander, Turner Ashby, killed in a skirmish on June 6, 1862 (Jackson wrote, "Poor Ashby is dead. He fell gloriously.''); the Confederates massed against first one

\begin{scenariodata}
Union force, then another, winning two battles in as many days. This\\
\end{scenariodata}

ended the Union pursuit, and Jackson was able to lead his army to Richmond, where their presence, and the absence of McDowell's Corps was to prove decisive.

"Stonewall" Jackson's 1862 campaign in the Shenandoah Valley is justifiably considered to be a masterpiece of maneuver, and has been favorably compared with Napoleon's early campaigns in Italy. He had

tied up a vastly superior Union army, won significant tactical victories over it, totally upset the Union strategic plan to take the Confederate Capital at Richmond, and saved the Shenandoah Valley to boot!
