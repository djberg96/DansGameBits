{\LARGE\bfseries Scenario 3 Example of Play\par}
{\large Harper's Ferry to Port Republic, Turn 1\par}
\vspace{0.25em}
\color{titleline}\rule{\textwidth}{1.2pt}\par
\vspace{0.45em}

This example follows one plausible full turn in Scenario 3 and is meant to illustrate command, movement, reaction, combat, retreat, and conclusion procedures. The die rolls are chosen for teaching purposes. It is grounded in the Scenario 3 setup and the later close-up map photos, especially the central valley road corridor linking Jackson's start area around hex 4511 to Banks's force in 4502.

\textbf{Starting situation.}

\begin{itemize}
\item Confederate: Jackson, Winder, Ewell, Ashby, and a wagon begin in hex 4511.
\item Union: Banks, Williams, Brodhead, and Detachment E begin in hex 4502; Saxton is in 4611; Fremont, Blenker, and Bayard are in 2516; Shields, Kimball, and Detachment F are in 3519.
\item Scenario 3 is a 1862 scenario, so the Confederate player gains the initiative on a die roll of 1--5.
\item The example assumes Jackson attacks northward down the main valley road toward Banks, while Fremont and Shields are slowed by weak command rolls and cannot intervene in time.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{1. Initial Phase}

There are no reinforcements and no replacements in this example turn, so the phase ends immediately.

\textbf{2. Initiative Phase}

The Confederate player rolls a \texttt{4}. In a 1862 scenario that gives the Confederates the initiative, so they will be the first player this turn.

\textbf{3. Confederate Command Phase}

Jackson is the only Confederate supreme commander on the map, so only one command-point die roll is needed. The Confederate player rolls a \texttt{5}. On the Command Point Table that gives Jackson \texttt{3} CPs for the turn.

Jackson's command rating is low enough that \texttt{3} CPs give him the full \texttt{100\%} of his printed movement allowance. Infantry and leaders normally have \texttt{8} MPs, so Jackson's force can use the full infantry allowance before stacking effects. Because Jackson received CPs at least equal to his command rating, he is also eligible to force march this turn.

\textbf{4. Confederate Movement Phase}

Jackson moves with Winder, Ewell, and Ashby as one force. Because more than two divisions are moving together, the force loses \texttt{1} MP for stacking under rule 7.31, so the moving force is treated as having \texttt{7} MPs. Jackson then force marches, adding MPs equal to his initiative. That brings the force to \texttt{10} MPs for the phase.

The Confederate wagon does not enter the combat hex. Instead, it follows the same road corridor and stops in a road hex two infantry MPs behind Banks. That keeps Jackson supplied for combat without forcing the wagon to keep up with the full march.

The Confederate player moves Jackson's force out of hex 4511 along the valley road toward Banks in 4502.

\begin{enumerate}
\item Most of the move uses road movement, so the force advances rapidly down the valley.
\item When Jackson enters the reaction zone of Banks's stack, the Union player declares a reaction attempt with Banks, Williams, Brodhead, and Detachment E.
\item Banks's initiative is poor, and the Union player fails the reaction die roll, so the stack remains in place.
\item Jackson then enters hex 4502. Because it contains enemy combat units, the Confederate force pays the extra \texttt{+1} MP required by rule 7.41 and may spend no more MPs that phase.
\end{enumerate}

At the end of Confederate movement, hex 4502 contains both sides, so a battle is now set up for the Combat Phase unless the Union can change the situation during its half of the turn.

\textbf{5. Union Command Phase}

The Union has three supreme commanders in the 1862 scenarios, so Banks, Fremont, and Shields each roll separately for command points.

\begin{itemize}
\item Banks rolls a \texttt{4} and receives \texttt{2} CPs.
\item Fremont rolls a \texttt{1} and receives only \texttt{1} CP.
\item Shields rolls a \texttt{2} and receives only \texttt{1} CP.
\end{itemize}

Banks therefore has enough command to fight decently in place, but Fremont and Shields both have weak movement allowances for the turn. Because commanders do not broadcast to units in their own hexes, each of these stacks simply uses the CP roll of its own senior commander.

\textbf{6. Union Movement Phase}

The Union player now decides what to do about Jackson's incursion.

\begin{itemize}
\item Banks is already pinned by Jackson in 4502. He could try to leave the enemy-occupied hex, but with weak initiative and an important position to hold, the Union player declines and prepares to fight.
\item Saxton stays in 4611.
\item Fremont's weak command roll gives Blenker and Bayard too little movement to reach 4502 from 2516.
\item Shields and Kimball are even farther away at 3519 and likewise cannot reinforce Banks this turn.
\end{itemize}

No other movement changes the impending battle, so the turn proceeds to combat.

\textbf{7. Combat Phase}

Only one battle must be resolved: hex 4502.

\textbf{Supply check.}

Both sides are supplied.

\begin{itemize}
\item Jackson's force can trace supply to the wagon left two infantry MPs behind it on the road.
\item Banks's force can use the wagon in 4502.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Combat options.}

Because Jackson is supplied, the Confederate player chooses \textit{Pitched Battle}. The Union player chooses \textit{Stand}. On the Combat Options Chart, that means the battle will last \texttt{2} rounds unless one side demoralizes sooner.

\textbf{Wagon depletion.}

Because Jackson chose \textit{Pitched Battle}, his wagon may deplete on a die roll of \texttt{5} or \texttt{6}. The Confederate player rolls a \texttt{3}, so the wagon does \textbf{not} deplete. The Union wagon also remains undepleted.

\textbf{Commitment.}

Each side now determines how much of its force can be committed.

\begin{itemize}
\item Jackson rolls \texttt{8} on 2d6 and adds \texttt{+1} for \textit{Pitched Battle}, for a modified commitment roll of \texttt{9}. On the CR 1 column that commits \texttt{100\%} of his force.
\item Banks rolls \texttt{5} on 2d6. Because the defenders did not move this turn, they receive the \texttt{+2} commitment modifier, for a modified \texttt{7}. On the CR 4 column that commits \texttt{60\%} of the defending force.
\end{itemize}

That gives:

\begin{itemize}
\item Confederate committed strength: Winder \texttt{24} + Ewell \texttt{22} + Ashby \texttt{3} = \texttt{49} SPs.
\item Union total strength in the hex is Williams \texttt{45} + Brodhead \texttt{3} + Detachment E \texttt{2} = \texttt{50} SPs.
\item At \texttt{60\%} commitment, the Union can commit \texttt{30} SPs. The Union player commits Williams for \texttt{27} SPs and Brodhead for \texttt{3} SPs, leaving Detachment E uncommitted.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Round 1.}

\begin{itemize}
\item Confederate fire: Jackson's combat bonus and \textit{Pitched Battle} produce a modified die roll of \texttt{7}. On the \texttt{46--55} column that inflicts \texttt{6} SP losses on the Union.
\item Union fire: the defender receives \texttt{+1} to fire and reaches a modified die roll of \texttt{5}. On the \texttt{26--35} column that inflicts \texttt{2} SP losses on the Confederates.
\end{itemize}

The losses are applied simultaneously. The Union has now lost more than \texttt{10\%} of its initial committed force, so it checks for demoralization at the end of the round.

\begin{itemize}
\item Union demoralization: the Union has lost \texttt{6} of \texttt{30} committed SPs, which is over \texttt{20\%}. Banks has a command rating of \texttt{4}, so the demoralization roll gets another \texttt{+1}. A die roll of \texttt{3} is modified to \texttt{6}, so the Union force becomes demoralized immediately.
\item Confederate demoralization is not checked because the battle stops as soon as the defending force demoralizes and retreats.
\end{itemize}

Because the Union demoralizes after the first round, the battle ends immediately even though the option pairing would otherwise have allowed a second round.

\textbf{8. Retreat}

The Union force in 4502 must retreat immediately. Under rule 14.21 it retreats \texttt{4} MPs without paying ZOC costs, and the Union player chooses a retreat route that falls back toward Fremont and Shields while avoiding forbidden enemy-occupied hexes and blocked exits.

Since the retreating side lost fewer than \texttt{15} SPs, the Confederate result is a \textbf{minor victory}, not a major victory.

Jackson remains in 4502 at the end of combat. The Confederate wagon is still a short distance to the rear on the road.

\textbf{9. Conclusion Phase}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Depletion and replenishment:} neither wagon depleted in this example, so both remain available next turn.
\item \textbf{Morale Recovery:} the Union may attempt to recover the demoralized force in the Morale Recovery Phase. In this example the recovery roll fails, so the force remains demoralized into the next turn.
\item \textbf{Union Panic Track:} because this is a 1862 scenario, the players now apply the appropriate Panic Track adjustment for the turn's result.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{What this example showed.}

\begin{itemize}
\item Initiative in a 1862 scenario
\item Command-point rolls and movement percentages
\item Force march
\item Stacking penalties during movement
\item Reaction movement failure
\item Entering an enemy-occupied hex
\item Supply traced to a trailing wagon
\item Combat options and commitment
\item Demoralization and retreat
\item End-of-turn morale recovery and the 1862 Panic Track step
\end{itemize}
