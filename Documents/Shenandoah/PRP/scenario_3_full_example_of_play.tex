{\LARGE\bfseries Scenario 3 Extended Example of Play\par}
{\large Harper's Ferry to Port Republic\par}
\vspace{0.25em}
\color{titleline}\rule{\textwidth}{1.2pt}\par
\vspace{0.45em}

The following example presents one plausible six-turn playthrough of Scenario 3. It is intended to follow the historical flow of the campaign from 29 May to 9 June 1862 as closely as the game system allows. The die rolls are chosen to produce a historically plausible result rather than a balanced competitive game. Where the available map images are not sharp enough to support a fully audited movement proof, the example uses the scenario's actual starting positions and the most likely road corridors.

\textbf{Purpose of the example.}

\begin{itemize}
\item Jackson avoids being trapped by the three Union columns converging on him.
\item Fremont, Shields, and Banks pursue, but poor timing prevents them from combining effectively.
\item The Confederates exploit interior lines and road movement while withdrawing south.
\item The final turns resemble Cross Keys and Port Republic: Ewell contains Fremont while Jackson defeats Shields.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Starting situation.}

\begin{itemize}
\item Confederate: Jackson, Winder, Ewell, Ashby, and a wagon begin in hex 4511.
\item Union: Banks, Williams, Brodhead, and Detachment E begin in hex 4502; Saxton is in 4611; Fremont, Blenker, and Bayard are in 2516; Shields, Kimball, and Detachment F are in 3519.
\item Scenario 3 lasts 6 turns. The Union Panic Track begins in box 4.
\item In a 1862 scenario the Confederates have the initiative on a die roll of 1--5.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Turn 1. Jackson begins the withdrawal south}

\textbf{Initiative.} The Confederate player rolls \texttt{4}; the Confederates move first.

\textbf{Confederate command and movement.}

Jackson rolls \texttt{5} for command and receives \texttt{3} CPs, enough for \texttt{100\%} movement allowance and a force march. He moves with Winder, Ewell, and Ashby as a single force. Stacking reduces the force by \texttt{1} MP, after which force march adds MPs equal to Jackson's initiative. The Confederate wagon trails behind on the road so that the column remains supplied without forcing the wagon to keep pace with the lead elements.

Instead of attacking Banks frontally, Jackson uses that command result to gain distance. He begins withdrawing south through the central valley road net, with Ashby screening and the infantry staying concentrated. Force-march attrition is then checked, and the Confederate roll is low enough that no strength is lost.

\textbf{Union command and movement.}

The Union rolls poorly:

\begin{itemize}
\item Banks rolls \texttt{4} for \texttt{2} CPs, giving his hex \texttt{50\%} movement: infantry/leaders \texttt{4} MPs, cavalry \texttt{5} MPs.
\item Fremont rolls \texttt{1} for \texttt{1} CP. With a command rating of \texttt{4}, that gives \texttt{25\%} movement: infantry/leaders \texttt{2} MPs, cavalry \texttt{3} MPs.
\item Shields rolls \texttt{2} for \texttt{1} CP. With a command rating of \texttt{3}, that gives about \texttt{33\%} movement: infantry/leaders \texttt{3} MPs, cavalry \texttt{3} MPs.
\end{itemize}

Banks retains some local mobility, but Fremont and Shields are too sluggish to form a coordinated net around Jackson.

\textbf{Result of the turn.}

No major battle occurs on Turn 1. That is appropriate for the historical situation. Jackson's object is not to reopen the lower-valley fighting but to avoid being fixed in place while Fremont and Shields converge. Banks advances cautiously behind him, while Fremont and Shields begin to close from their separate approaches.

\textbf{Conclusion.}

Jackson has preserved freedom of action. The Union columns remain separated.

\textbf{Turn 2. Jackson breaks contact and turns south}

\textbf{Initiative.} Confederate roll \texttt{3}; the Confederates move first again.

\textbf{Confederate plan.}

Jackson continues to slip away before the converging Union columns can close. Ashby screens to the rear while Jackson, Winder, and Ewell move south along the valley road network.

\textbf{Command and movement.}

Jackson receives a solid but not spectacular command result, enough for near-full movement. The wagon follows behind the main body rather than racing ahead. The Confederate force uses road movement to gain distance while remaining concentrated.

\textbf{Union half of the turn.}

Banks remains more concerned with maintaining contact than with forcing an immediate battle. Fremont and Shields both receive better command rolls than on Turn 1, but not enough to coordinate a true double envelopment. Fremont begins pressing from the west side of the valley, while Shields moves northward from the Luray side.

\textbf{Operational result.}

By the end of Turn 2, the Union threat is broad but still not synchronized. Jackson has traded ground for time and remains free to choose where he will stand.

\textbf{Turn 3. Ashby delays while Jackson concentrates}

\textbf{Initiative.} Confederate roll \texttt{5}; Confederates still move first.

\textbf{Confederate command.}

Jackson receives enough CPs to keep the army moving. Ashby is used aggressively as a cavalry screen. Winder and Ewell are kept close enough to support one another rather than being scattered in detachments.

\textbf{Movement.}

\begin{itemize}
\item Ashby takes up a delaying position along the western approach, threatening reaction and forcing Fremont to advance carefully.
\item Jackson, Winder, and the wagon continue south through the central valley.
\item Ewell is positioned where he can either support Jackson or turn west to face Fremont if required.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Union response.}

Union command improves this turn, but geography still matters. Fremont can move, but his western route is slower and more awkward. Shields can move, but he is operating on the eastern side and still cannot combine directly with Fremont. Banks is no longer the principal striking force and increasingly becomes a secondary factor.

\textbf{Combat.}

Only light cavalry skirmishing, or no decisive battle at all, occurs this turn. That is appropriate. The middle phase of the campaign is defined more by delay, concealment, and concentration than by immediate general engagement.

\textbf{Turn 4. The Union closes, but too late}

\textbf{Initiative.} Confederate roll \texttt{2}; Confederates again seize the first move.

\textbf{Confederate posture.}

Jackson's army now pivots toward the Harrisonburg--Cross Keys--Port Republic area. The Confederates adopt a classic interior-lines posture:

\begin{itemize}
\item Ewell shades west toward Fremont.
\item Winder remains closer to Jackson and the road to Port Republic.
\item The wagon stays central enough to keep the army supplied for decisive action in the last two turns.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Union posture.}

The Union player finally has the rough shape of a trap: Fremont from one side, Shields from the other, with Banks still applying pressure from the rear. But the timing still does not work. Their columns are close enough to threaten, not close enough to combine in the same battle on the same turn. This is precisely the strategic failure Jackson historically exploited.

\textbf{Local action.}

Ashby and the Confederate rear guard continue to slow Fremont. Even where no major combat occurs, the threat of reaction, enemy occupation, and terrain costs forces the Union player to spend movement inefficiently.

\textbf{Turn 5. Cross Keys}

\textbf{Initiative.} Confederate roll \texttt{4}; Confederates move first.

\textbf{Confederate design.}

This is the turn on which the historical analogy becomes strongest. Ewell is used to face Fremont while Jackson and Winder prepare to strike Shields on the following day. The Confederate player is willing to accept a holding battle in the west if it prevents Union coordination.

\textbf{Command and movement.}

Jackson's roll is adequate, not brilliant. That is enough. Ewell occupies a strong blocking position against Fremont, with Ashby supporting where cavalry can assist with reaction and screening.

\textbf{Union command.}

Fremont at last has enough movement to come into contact in strength. Shields also advances, but not in time to combine with Fremont's battle. The Union player must therefore fight piecemeal.

\textbf{Combat at Cross Keys style position.}

Ewell accepts battle against Fremont. The Confederate player chooses a more economical combat option than would be used in an annihilating attack, because the object here is to hold, not to destroy. Fremont attacks, but terrain, commitment limits, and Confederate concentration prevent a breakthrough. The Union suffers enough loss and delay that it cannot exploit west of the valley this turn.

\textbf{Operational result.}

By the end of Turn 5, Fremont has been checked. Jackson has gained exactly what he wanted: one more day to fall on Shields without effective interference from the west.

\textbf{Turn 6. Port Republic}

\textbf{Initiative.} Confederate roll \texttt{1}; Confederates still move first at the decisive moment.

\textbf{Confederate plan.}

Jackson now turns his full striking power against Shields. Ewell is left to contain Fremont as economically as possible, while Jackson and Winder mass for the final blow. The wagon is kept close enough to support an all-out attack.

\textbf{Union position.}

Shields is advanced enough to fight, but not supported enough to survive a concentrated Confederate assault. His command roll is serviceable, yet not high enough to produce the level of movement and commitment needed either to escape or to be reinforced in time.

\textbf{Movement and battle.}

Jackson force marches if necessary to close the distance, accepting the attrition risk because this is the last turn and the historical battle demands decisive action. The Confederates strike Shields in a supplied major battle. Jackson chooses \textit{Pitched Battle}; the Union must either stand on unfavorable terms or withdraw and concede the field. In this example Shields stands.

Jackson and Winder commit heavily. Shields commits a respectable portion of his force, but not enough to match Confederate concentration. Confederate fire wins the exchange. The Union either demoralizes during the battle or is forced into a damaging retreat after a sharp final round. The result is a clear Confederate success at the scenario's climax.

\textbf{End of game result.}

The six-turn arc now resembles the historical campaign:

\begin{itemize}
\item Jackson withdraws south before Fremont, Shields, and Banks can combine.
\item Ashby and the Confederate rear guard delay the pursuit.
\item Fremont is checked in a holding action resembling Cross Keys.
\item Shields is beaten in the final decisive fight resembling Port Republic.
\end{itemize}

That sequence should produce a favorable Panic Track and victory-point outcome for the Confederates, most likely a substantive or overwhelming Confederate victory depending on the exact Panic adjustments and battle-victory awards recorded during play.

\textbf{What this example illustrates.}

\begin{itemize}
\item Scenario 3 is not simply a race to battle; it is a campaign of timing, roads, and concentration.
\item Superior Confederate command on the critical turns lets Jackson avoid entrapment and fight one Union column at a time.
\item Weak Union command does not merely reduce movement; it destroys coordination.
\item The wagon rules matter operationally: keeping supply close without tying the wagon to the lead combat hex is often the right play.
\item The historical Confederate result comes from knowing when to retreat, when to delay, and when finally to force a major battle.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Design note.}

This example is intentionally more narrative than the separate single-turn teaching example. The shorter piece is better for learning the exact order of procedures. This longer version is better for understanding how those procedures combine into a historically plausible six-turn campaign.
